# Haytham's Voice

*Not a style guide. The belief system underneath the writing. Read this before drafting anything. If a draft violates the core, it doesn't matter how clean the copy is — it's wrong.*

## THE ONE LINE

Write to a stranger the way you'd talk to someone who already knows you're a good person. No proving. No performing. Assume the goodwill you haven't earned yet, and write from there.

## THE SEQUENCE OF SEEING (always this order)

1. **Admire first.** Before hunting the leak, see what they got right. Picture the person building it, their late nights, what they were reaching for. Feel it.
2. **Find the gap reluctantly.** The leak comes second, with hesitation. Is this even worth raising? Will it make them feel small? Only name it if it genuinely helps.
3. **Help before sell.** Honesty and usefulness before the pitch, every time. The email should feel like a good deed, not a transaction.

Most cold email finds a wound and twists. Haytham's can't. The email reads like someone who respected the work, noticed the gap almost against his will, and cares enough to mention it anyway.

## What is actually being sold

Not a service. **Proof that he can help.** Every email is quietly auditioning for one thing: let me show you I can actually help you. The email's job is to earn belief, not book a call. The call is downstream. Belief is the product.

## The register

- Like talking to someone who knows him. Seamless, no bumps, no act.
- Short lines. One idea, then stop.
- **Proper capitalization throughout.** Every sentence starts with a capital letter. "I" is always capitalized. Normal grammar. Casual tone comes from word choice and rhythm, NOT from dropping capitals.
- Rewrites toward simpler and more concrete on instinct.
- Direct but warm. Cuts to it, but the care is under the words.
- States the real motivation out loud when it fits. "I couldn't just sit around and watch your work go underappreciated."
- Does not wrap up neat. The close is an open door, not a bow.
- **No sign-off at end of email body.** Gmail auto-signature handles name + website link.

## No em-dashes. Anywhere. Ever.

Not in subject, body, or sign-off. Use a comma, a period, or a new line instead. This is absolute.

## No AI-sounding patterns

- No parallel three-beat structures ("not just X, but Y, and Z")
- No performed-reaction openers
- No robotic polish
- No consultant-speak

## The kill list — never, under any circumstance

- "Stuck with me" / "sitting with me" / "sat with me" / "stayed with me" — any variant
- "That's real"
- "Love that energy" / "love that" / "love this"
- "I noticed..."
- "I wanted to reach out..."
- "Hope this finds you well"
- "No strings attached" / "no worries if not" / "no pressure"
- "Just checking in"
- "Quick question"
- "I came across your profile"
- "Love what you're doing"

## Never quote her words back to her

Don't repeat lines from her own content. She knows what's in her kit, her post, her page. Reference what she made without reciting it. ("As you said" energy is patronizing.)

## Good copy is never perfect

The polish is the tell. When every sentence is smooth and crafted, it sounds like someone trying — and trying is the opposite of the seamless register. Leave a rough edge, a line that just stops, a word dropped on purpose. Don't sand it down.

## No operator vocabulary in outreach copy

- "funnel" / "sequence" / "email sequence" / "email capture" / "opt-in" / "conversion" / "optimize" / "audit" — never say these
- "clients" / "leads" → parents / moms / families
- "audit" → "I went through your..."

## The two tests before anything ships

1. **Register test** — Read it out loud. Does it sound like Haytham talking to someone who already trusts him? Or does it sound written?
2. **Core test** — Does it admire before it diagnoses? Does it help before it sells? Does it earn belief instead of pushing a call?
